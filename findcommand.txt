If curl and wget are for getting things from the web, find is for searching through everything on your own computer.
It's essentially a high-powered "Search Bar" for your terminal. [1, 2] 
## The Basic Formula

find [where to look] -name "what to look for"

## The Most Helpful Commands

   1. Search by Name
   Find a specific file anywhere in your current folder and its subfolders:
   find . -name "my_file.txt"
   (The . means "start here.")
   2. Case-Insensitive Search
   If you aren't sure if it was capitalized (like Report.txt vs report.txt):
   find . -iname "report.txt"
   3. Search by File Type
   Find only folders (directories) with a certain name:
   find . -type d -name "work"
   Or only regular files:
   find . -type f -name "*.jpg"
   4. Search by Size (The "Space-Saver")
   Find files larger than 100MB to see what's eating your disk space:
   find . -size +100M
   5. Search by Time
   Find files modified in the last 24 hours:
   find . -mtime -1 [1, 3, 4, 5, 6]

## When to use "Find" vs "Grep"
It's easy to mix these up, but here is the rule of thumb: [7, 8] 

* Use find when you are looking for the file itself (based on its name, size, or date).
* Use grep when you are looking for text inside a file. [1, 2, 3, 7, 9] 

Quick Example:
If you want to find all .log files and search them for the word "Error":
find . -name "*.log" -exec grep "Error" {} \; [3, 10] 
Are you trying to track down a lost file, or are you looking to clean up some space on your drive?
